    ;

By the algorithm described in the corollary of Claim 2 in the linked paper by Chaurasia, Lodhi, and Arora, we can show that $\phi_2$ is satisfiable.

Starting from $x_1$, we cannot reach $\overline{x_1}$. Assign $x_1 = T, \overline{x_1} = F$.

From $x_1$, we can reach $x_2$. Assign $x_2 = T$ and assign its negation $\overline{x_2} = F$.

From $x_2$ we can reach $\overline{x_3}$. Assign $\overline{x_3} = T$ and assign its negation $x_3 = F$.

Now we have provided assignments for all the nodes.

The following values satisfy $\phi_2$:

\begin{align*}
  x_1 &= T & \overline{x_1} = F \\
  x_2 &= T & \overline{x_2} = F \\
  x_3 &= F & \overline{x_3} = T \\
\end{align*}

Substituting into $\phi_2$:

\begin{align*}
  && \\
  \phi_2 & = (\overline{x_1} \vee x_2) &&\wedge&& ( x_2  \vee x_3) &&\wedge&&  (\overline{x_3} \vee \overline{x_2}) &&\wedge&& (x_1 \vee \overline{x_3}) &&\wedge&& (x_1 \vee x_3) \\
  & = (\overline{x_1} \vee T) &&\wedge&& ( T  \vee x_3) &&\wedge&&  (T \vee \overline{x_2}) &&\wedge&& (T \vee \overline{x_3}) &&\wedge&& (T \vee x_3)\\
  & = (T) &&\wedge&& (T) &&\wedge&&  (T) &&\wedge&& (T) &&\wedge&& (T) \\
  & = T
\end{align*}
